## Week 2 — from "Why AI Hasn't Made Your Business Faster Yet"

### 4 · Mon Jul 27, 8:30 AM — Task Time vs. Coordination Time

> Break any piece of work in your business into two costs.
>
> Task time: drafting the email, building the estimate, writing the
> summary.
>
> Coordination time: figuring out whose job it is, waiting for the
> info it needs, handing it to the next person, deciding if it's good
> enough, confirming it actually happened.
>
> In any business with more than a couple of people, coordination time
> dominates. The estimate takes forty minutes to build and three days
> to move.
>
> Here's the problem: AI compresses task time. It does almost nothing
> for coordination time — because coordination isn't a writing
> problem. It's a structure problem. Who owns this. What happens next.
> Who decides.
>
> So when you adopt AI tool-by-tool, you speed up the forty minutes
> and leave the three days untouched. You can compress task time to
> ZERO and the work still takes three days.
>
> That's why everyone in your business is "using AI" and your week
> isn't any lighter. The bottleneck was never the part AI accelerates.
>
> I wrote up the full argument — including what to do about it this
> week — here:
> https://shankstrategy.com/blog/why-ai-hasnt-made-your-business-faster-yet

### 5 · Wed Jul 29, 8:30 AM — Bottleneck of the Week #2 (no link)

> Bottleneck of the week: the ninety-percent-done pile.
>
> Every stuck operation I look at has one. The proposal that's
> "basically ready." The process that's "mostly rolled out." The hire
> that's "nearly onboarded."
>
> Work stalls at ninety percent for a boring reason: nobody defined
> the last ten. "Done" means "when it feels done" — and nothing ever
> quite feels done, so it hovers.
>
> The ninety-percent pile is expensive twice. The work delivers zero
> value while it hovers — a proposal that's basically ready closes
> exactly as many deals as one that doesn't exist. And it occupies
> attention the entire time. Every "where are we on that?" is a tax.
>
> The fix isn't motivation. It's definition: a version of done that
> someone can verify. Sent or not sent. Live or not live. Signed or
> not signed.
>
> An operation with crisp definitions of done feels lighter at the
> same workload. Not because anyone works harder — because nothing
> hovers.
>
> What's been sitting at ninety percent in your business the longest?

### 6 · Fri Jul 31, 8:30 AM — Why the Pilot Didn't Stick

> The AI pilot arc, as it actually happens in small businesses:
>
> Someone champions a tool. The demo impresses. Three months later,
> usage has decayed back to the one person who personally likes it.
>
> The post-mortem blames the tool, or the team's "resistance to
> change." But look at what the pilot actually tested.
>
> It tested whether the tool works. The tool almost always works.
>
> What it never tested: whether a named person owns the output.
> Whether the handoff into and out of the AI step is defined. Whether
> anyone agreed what "working" means on a bad week, with a real
> customer waiting.
>
> A demo runs under demo conditions. An operation runs under live
> conditions. A pilot with no owner and no exit standard isn't a test.
> It's a rehearsal for the decay.
>

## Week 3 — from "The Founder Bottleneck: Six Questions"

### 7 · Mon Aug 3, 8:30 AM — The Six Questions

> Six questions that find where work actually gets stuck in a small
> business. Answer them in writing, with recent examples — vague
> answers are themselves data.
>
> 1. Where do decisions wait? Not the analysis — the waiting, after
> the information is available.
>
> 2. Where does work change hands, and what happens to it there? Did
> the receiver know it was theirs? Did anything need re-explaining or
> chasing?
>
> 3. What gets discussed more than once? A topic discussed twice
> without changing isn't being discussed. It's being deferred with
> ceremony.
>
> 4. What can only you touch? List what stops moving when you're
> unavailable for three days. The honest list is longer than the
> flattering one.
>
> 5. Where does "done" go blurry? Find what's chronically ninety
> percent finished.
>
> 6. Where is AI producing work that no named person stands behind?
> Every blank on that list is drift wearing a good suit.
>
> In founder-led businesses, the honest answer to several of these is
> usually the founder. Not incompetence — the business grew around
> you, and nobody re-routed the paths.
>
> Full walkthrough, including how to read your answers:
> https://shankstrategy.com/blog/the-founder-bottleneck-six-questions

### 8 · Wed Aug 5, 8:30 AM — Bottleneck of the Week #3 (no link)

> Bottleneck of the week: the three-day test.
>
> List everything in your business that stops moving when you're
> unavailable for three days.
>
> Quotes? Approvals? The reply a client is waiting on? Payroll? The
> answer to a technical question that lives only in your head?
>

> the person who could do everything, and nobody re-drew the paths
> when the volume outgrew the person.
>
> Here's the tell: the items you'd defend hardest as "faster if I just
> do it myself" are usually the ones most worth moving. "Faster if I
> do it" is true for every individual instance and catastrophic as a
> policy.
>
> The fix isn't delegation as a virtue. It's moving specific decision
> rights — with the authority to be wrong — one category at a time.
